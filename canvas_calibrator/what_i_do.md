# What I Do

Canvas Calibrator works by ingesting all of the readable content in an online course — including page text, uploaded PDFs, and linked external resources — and using that material as the sole "textbook" for a simulated learner. That learner is powered by a Large Language Model (LLM): a system that reads and understands text the way a person would, but is strictly limited to answering only from what the course itself provides. It does not draw on outside knowledge. For each quiz question, the LLM searches the course material for relevant passages, then selects what it believes is the correct answer — just as a diligent student would before an exam.

In this initial test, the calibrator was run against **CHRM200-BPM**, a Human Resources Management course containing 22 quizzes and 198 total questions. The LLM learner answered correctly on 73 questions (37%) where the course material clearly supported the right answer. On 10 additional questions (5%), it found relevant material but was led to an incorrect answer — suggesting the content may be ambiguous or incomplete on those topics. The most significant finding: **115 questions (58%) were entirely unsupported** — the learner could not find any course material addressing the concept being tested. These ranged from foundational HR terms like *Human Resources Management*, *Human Capital*, and *Workforce Analytics*, to specific regulatory facts such as BC's minimum wage and minimum working age.

This 42% calibration score is a strong signal that the course's quiz questions are drawing on knowledge not yet present in the course readings. Whether that content is embedded in video lectures, assumed as prior knowledge, or simply missing from the course package, the calibration report gives course designers a clear, prioritized list of gaps to address — making it a practical tool for ensuring that what is tested is actually taught.
